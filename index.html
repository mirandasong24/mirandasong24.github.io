Does music really fit into genres?¶

By Hannah Darrow and Miranda Song

Introduction¶

Musical genres are an important aspect of classifying music. We searched for music-related databases on Kaggle and found several related to genre classification. Our selected dataset contains numerical data and categorizes each song by genre. We chose this dataset so we could use the numerical data to predict genre. Most other music datasets did not include genre labels. In the original dataset "data.csv," there existed ten unique labels - blues, classical, country, disco, hiphop, jazz, metal, pop, reggae, and rock. We decided to choose five of the ten labels to decrease the number of classes on which to train the algorithms, and specifically, we chose the five labels classical, country, disco, pop, and jazz because we made a qualitative prediction that these music genres were the most distinct out of the ten provided by the original dataset. We chose the tempo, chroma_stft, rolloff, and zero crossing rate as potential independent variables to predict the tempo of a song because these metrics have relevance to the content of the song itself, and so we projected that they could be important indicators to predicting the correct genre of the song. Specifically, the tempo of the song is the speed at which a passage of music is or should be played, the chroma stft is a short-term Fourier transformation to compute Chroma features and represents information about the classification of pitch and signal structure, the rolloff in a song is the smooth fall of response to zero at either end of the frequency range of a piece of audio equipment, and the zero crossing rate of the song is the rate at which a signal changes from positive to zero to negative or from negative to zero to positive. Thus, with these descriptions, it is apparent that these columns are relevant to the construction of the song and thus could potentially help predict the genre of the song.

top_songs = pd.DataFrame(columns=['tempo', 'chroma_stft', 'rolloff', 'zero_crossing_rate', 'label', 'title'])
for filename in os.listdir('./top_100/'):
    song = f'./top_100/{filename}'
    data, sampling_rate = librosa.load(song, mono=True, duration=30)
    tempo, beats = librosa.beat.beat_track(y=data, sr=sampling_rate)
    chroma_stft = librosa.feature.chroma_stft(y=data, sr=sampling_rate)
    rolloff = librosa.feature.spectral_rolloff(y=data, sr=sampling_rate)
    zero_crossing_rate = librosa.feature.zero_crossing_rate(data)
    top_songs.loc[len(top_songs.index)] = [tempo, np.mean(chroma_stft), np.mean(rolloff), np.mean(zero_crossing_rate), filename, f'{filename}'.replace(".wav", "")]

Since genre is not obtainable through the librosa package, we used the spotipy package to obtain the artist genres for each artist in our playlist. Spotify does not provide genre information for individual songs, but the artist's genre is a good estimator of the genre of the song. We then combined the genres with the data gathered using librosa.

First, we decided to perform the linear regression analysis between the tempo of the song and the genre of the song. We can see from the OLS regression output that the intercept itself is the classical genre, and comparatively, all other genres including country, disco, jazz, and pop, have slower tempos than the classical genre. We find this interesting and logical, as although the classical music genre tends to be known for a slower pace, it does have variation in its pieces/songs, and so this regression result indicates to us that this dataset likely has classical songs that are more fast-paced than its counterparts in other categories.

We can see that the increasing order of tempo of the songs by genre looks like 1) classical, 2) disco, 3) jazz, 4) pop, and 5) country. This conclusion corresponds to the violin plots illustrated in the previous section, where we see visually that the spread of classical songs in this dataset tends to stretch to slightly higher tempos than the songs in the other genres. This ordering of the songs will serve as a baseline when looking at the remaining three variables (chroma_stft, rolloff, and zero crossing rate) that could help predict the tempo of the song.

Further Analysis and Conclusion¶

Music is hardly fit for black-and-white classification for genre. However, we decided to take the challenge and explore if there really did exist any relationships between how the rolloff of a song could predict its tempo in conjunction with its genre, or whether the combination of the genre and zero crossing rate of a song could predict the beats per minute of a song. Of course, there exist many more genres than the five we decided to explore in this dataset. In fact, as aforementioned, the dataset itself had ten genres, including the five genres we didn't explore - blues, hiphop, metal, reggae, and rock. The introduction of more genres increases the difficulty of the classification problem - what factor(s) exactly influence or define the genre of a song? What can we say about the similarities in genre, and who is to say that one song cannot be in two or more genres at once? With these questions in mind, we may ponder that even though we may attempt to classify songs into various categories, at the end of the day, music is an expression of art!
